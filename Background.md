# Background: The University vs. Trades Decision

## Why This Decision Matters

High school guidance counselors in Nova Scotia occupy a pivotal position in shaping the province's future workforce and their students' financial trajectories. The guidance they provide has never been more consequential. Nova Scotia faces a critical skilled trades shortage, with the province needing 11,000 new tradespeople by 2030 to meet construction and infrastructure demands (Government of Nova Scotia, 2023). This labour gap is already causing significant economic damage—in 2022 alone, Nova Scotia businesses missed out on approximately $1 billion worth of potential sales and contracts due to insufficient skilled workers (Gorman, 2023). Thirty-five percent of current skilled trades workers are over the age of 55, meaning a massive wave of retirements will further exacerbate the shortage within the next decade (Grillo de Lambarri, 2025).

Meanwhile, university graduates face a different but equally serious challenge: student debt. The average Canadian university graduate carries between $28,000 and $29,000 in student-related debt, taking an average of 10 years to repay these loans (Debt.ca, 2025). More than half of bachelor's degree recipients graduate with debt, and this financial burden significantly impacts young adults' ability to purchase homes, start families, save for retirement, or take entrepreneurial risks. For students from low-income backgrounds, debt aversion is a primary barrier to pursuing post-secondary education altogether.

## What Makes This Decision Difficult

The tension counselors face stems from a complex interplay of cultural expectations, economic realities, and individual student circumstances. For decades, Nova Scotia's education system has predominantly promoted university as the pathway to success. As Duncan Williams, president and CEO of the Construction Association of Nova Scotia, notes, "We've had an entire education system that's largely been focused on promoting university and university is a great option, but it's not an option for everybody" (Canadian Broadcasting Corporation, 2022). This cultural bias creates strong parental expectations and social prestige associations that counselors must navigate carefully.

The earnings picture adds complexity rather than clarity. While Statistics Canada data shows bachelor's degree holders earn a median of $48,000 two years after graduation compared to $35,000 for college graduates overall, specific trades dramatically outperform many university degrees (Statistics Canada, 2020). College graduates in mechanic and repair technologies earn $55,600 median income, while construction trades workers earn $54,100—figures that exceed many bachelor's degree outcomes while avoiding substantial debt accumulation. However, lifetime earnings trajectories, career flexibility, and opportunities for advancement remain important considerations that vary significantly by field and individual aptitude.

The problem is further complicated by historical policy decisions. In 1996, Nova Scotia amalgamated its vocational schools into the Nova Scotia Community College and began charging tuition for training that was previously free, inadvertently reinforcing the perception that trades represent a "second choice" rather than a strategic career decision (Canadian Broadcasting Corporation, 2022). The province has since recognized this error and invested $100 million over three years to accelerate skilled trades growth, including the More Opportunity for Skilled Trades (MOST) tax rebate program that returns provincial income tax on the first $50,000 of income for workers under 30 in eligible trades (Government of Nova Scotia, 2023).

## Key Stakeholders

This decision affects multiple stakeholders with competing interests and perspectives:

**Students** are the primary stakeholders whose futures hang in the balance. Their career satisfaction, earning potential, debt levels, and quality of life for decades to come depend significantly on the guidance they receive. Each student brings unique academic strengths, learning preferences, hands-on aptitudes, and career aspirations that should inform individualized recommendations.

**Parents** often hold strong expectations that their children attend university, viewing it as a marker of success and upward mobility. Many parents lack awareness of current labour market realities, skilled trades earnings potential, or the financial burden of student debt. Counselors must educate and sometimes challenge parental assumptions while respecting family dynamics.

**Employers** face severe labour shortages in skilled trades while also needing university-educated workers in fields like healthcare, education, and technology. The Construction Association of Nova Scotia and other industry groups actively advocate for greater emphasis on trades education and apprenticeship pathways.

**Educational institutions** have vested interests in enrollment patterns. The Nova Scotia Community College benefits from increased trades enrollment, while universities rely on maintaining steady student numbers for financial sustainability. Both institutions provide valuable educational pathways but compete for the same pool of graduating high school students.

**The Nova Scotia economy** suffers directly from the skilled trades shortage, with delayed infrastructure projects, housing construction bottlenecks, and businesses unable to expand. At the same time, the province needs university graduates in critical fields. The balance between these needs affects economic growth, housing affordability, and overall provincial competitiveness.

**The guidance counselor** bears professional and ethical responsibility for providing sound advice while managing institutional pressures, parental expectations, and personal biases. Counselors' reputations and job satisfaction depend partly on their students' post-secondary outcomes, creating incentives that may not always align with individual student needs.

## What Has Been Tried Before

Historically, Nova Scotia's guidance counselors operated within a system that strongly emphasized university pathways, particularly for academically strong students. Vocational and trades education was often positioned as an alternative for students who struggled academically or lacked interest in traditional classroom learning. This approach reflected broader societal values but also stemmed from the practical reality that vocational training was freely available through provincial vocational schools prior to 1996.

The 1996 amalgamation of vocational schools into NSCC and the introduction of tuition fees fundamentally altered the landscape. Students who once could access free trades training now faced costs comparable to university education without the social prestige associated with a degree. This policy shift, while intended to modernize vocational education, inadvertently reinforced the hierarchy between university and trades pathways.

In recent years, awareness of the skilled trades shortage has grown, prompting both government and industry responses. The Nova Scotia government launched the More Opportunity for Skilled Trades (MOST) program, which returns provincial income tax paid on the first $50,000 of income for workers under 30 in eligible trades (Government of Nova Scotia, 2023). The province also changed apprenticeship ratios, increased funding for trades training, and created specialized immigration streams to attract skilled workers from abroad.

Marketing campaigns have attempted to rebrand skilled trades as lucrative, stable careers. The Nova Scotia Apprenticeship Agency has worked to increase diversity in trades, recognizing that women, Indigenous people, Black Nova Scotians, immigrants, and people with disabilities remain significantly underrepresented. Despite these efforts, cultural perceptions change slowly, and many counselors continue to default to university recommendations for their strongest students.

Current best practice in guidance counseling emphasizes individualized decision-making based on student aptitudes, interests, learning styles, and career goals rather than one-size-fits-all recommendations. However, counselors face time constraints—often only 2-3 sessions per student—making it difficult to conduct thorough assessments and provide comprehensive information about all pathways. The challenge moving forward is developing frameworks that allow counselors to efficiently match students with optimal pathways while challenging ingrained assumptions about what constitutes educational success.
